# 📊 COVID-19 Death Data Visualization Dashboard
### DSC651 — Group DATAMAZING | UiTM Shah Alam

> An interactive Power BI dashboard that visualizes COVID-19 death trends in Malaysia, analyzing the impact of vaccination status, demographics, comorbidities, and geography on mortality outcomes.

---

## 🎯 Project Aim

To analyze and visualize COVID-19 death data in Malaysia using demographic variables (age, gender, state), comorbidities, and vaccination status — helping health authorities, researchers, and the public understand key risk factors and mortality trends.

---

## ❓ Research Questions

1. How did COVID-19 death trends vary across different **states** in Malaysia?
2. How did **comorbidities and age** influence death rates across age groups?
3. What role did **gender, comorbidities, and brought-in-dead (BID) status** play in COVID-19 deaths?
4. How did **vaccination rates** impact death trends over time?
5. How did death distribution compare between **Malaysian and non-Malaysian** citizens?

---

## 📂 Dataset Variables

| Category | Variables |
|---|---|
| **Demographics** | Age, gender, nationality, state |
| **Vaccination** | Vaccine brand (1st, 2nd, 3rd dose), vaccination status (unvaccinated / partially / fully / boosted) |
| **Health Info** | Comorbidities, BID (brought-in-dead) status |
| **Dates** | Date of death, date of announcement, date tested positive, dates of each vaccine dose |

---

## 🔍 Key Findings

- **Unvaccinated individuals** faced significantly higher mortality risk, especially among the elderly and those with comorbidities
- **Booster doses** were associated with a marked decline in deaths during the Omicron wave
- **Senior citizens aged 60 and above** consistently showed higher mortality due to weakened immune systems and higher comorbidity rates
- **Common comorbidities** linked to deaths: diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, chronic lung conditions
- **Rural and lower-income populations** experienced higher death rates due to limited vaccine access and healthcare services
- **Male deaths** were disproportionately higher than female, suggesting biological and behavioral factors
